SAMIRA

of the Exiled Lands  ·  formerly of central Shem

✦ ✦ ✦

Loss becomes her.
Born to the Beni Laleh, of the Beni Zarif.
Devoted to her clan, her Patron Derketo,
to the inhales and exhales of the tapestry of life.
Exiled by the betrayal of a friend and wanted love,
by nobility who were strangers to her,
by the twisting of truth and law.

Origins

Samira was born eight and twenty years ago to the Beni Laleh — a clan of the Beni Zarif tribe, settled in the plateaued scrublands of central Shem, north of Shumir. Their lands were sparse, but sustainable: stretches of desert broken by rock formations, patches of greenery, and the occasional small oasis, situated near the mountain passage connecting Shem to Koth above. The Beni Zarif were not the city-dwelling Shemites of the western coast, nor the fierce Zuagir raiders of the eastern desert — they occupied a middle ground, loosely nomadic, sustained by trade, craftsmanship, and the favor of their Gods.

Her tribe was known for their beauty, their wit, the rare tulips that dotted their lands, and their prowess with the bow. The Beni Laleh distinguished themselves as they claimed their clan-ground surrounding a concealed sanctuary to Derketo — a low jut of rock that opened, through a narrow hunched passage, into an impressive cavern draped with rich tapestry, lit by torches, and centered on a gilded limestone carving of the Mistress. Pilgrims came from Shumir and beyond to observe rites there. The shrine, and the rumors that clung to it, were the heartbeat of the clan's livelihood and reputation both.

Family & Formation

She was the second child of six, eldest daughter of a man of notable influence within the clan — a trader, a rider, a lover of spiced wine and open roads, generous in his protection and his wisdom. Her mother was a mellow, soothing presence, with a penchant for story-telling: well-read for a woman outside the cities, devoted to her children and the thrum of clan chores, and one who saw the spirit in every living thing. It was from her mother that Samira first understood that beauty was not ornament, but attentiveness.

Her eldest brother — only two years her senior and her closest companion in childhood — shaped her perhaps more than anyone. They learned together: to ride, to fletch arrows, of mischief, of fending off a pack of hyenas with sticks and nerve when they strayed too far from the tents as kids. She was his shadow. He debated with the man who mentored him the most on her behalf, winning her the right to learn blades with them as well as the bow. He died on the cusp of his sixteenth year, when a band thieving travelers exploited the clan's hospitality by way of familiarity in a single catastrophic morning. Around a third of the clan's men were lost alongside him, including one of their uncles as well as the mentor who had taught them both. The Beni Zarif did not let a single man leave their borders. Samira was fourteen.

In the grief that followed, her father drew her closer. She was unsure if it was because she was now his oldest, or because of all her siblings she reminded him most of the son he had lost. The years between fourteen and twenty-five were spent at his side on trade routes to Shumir and across the desert — learning the ways of commerce, of self-defense, of reading people and places. He continued in the lessons her mentor had begun: not only blades and survival, but charm, wit, and how to guide a conversation to where you needed it to go. By the time she was a woman of standing in her clan, she carried with her the knowledge of many; if asked, she would name them as the most influential.

Faith & Philosophy

Derketo is not merely Samira's patron — she is the architecture of the way Samira moves through the world. The goddess of life and death asks nothing so simple as worship. She asks that her devotees hold both truths at once: that the world is beautiful and brutal, that the leopard and the gazelle are equally beloved, that what dies does not end. Samira's theology was supported by ritual and shrine, and confirmed for her in lived experience — the sting of the scorpion, in the dew that the mountain air left on the scrubland brush at night, in the chips on her idol she refuses to repair, and most notably - Najee - the serpent she bonded with at twelve.

That idol — an ivory figure carved for her by her mother's aunt, gifted in her seventh summer — is with her always. Through... "creative" means, she managed to bring this one object with her, hidden in warmth where she prayed no guards would explore. A tall woman in thin cloth, a king cobra wound from foot to waist, its hooded head raised at her bared throat as if in vigil. Worn heavily in places, chipped in others. She will not restore the damage. To fight decay, she believes, would be to deceive herself in a way the Mistress would not forgive. It is her most precious possession, and she holds it to be intertwined with her spirit and with Derketo's own.

Many other theologies rely on broader faith in power oft unseen, whereas life and death is bittersweet truth. Derketo leads not only her people of birth, but her heart, and while she never claims to know any of the Gods full truth - their intents - she believes that her intuition is granted and guided by something higher, and trusts it above any being.

On her person — always

A figure of tall beauty, with long straight hair and a hooked nose, wrapped by a calm king cobra with it’s hood down, laying in a bed of reeds and lotus blooms. The woman has her face turned to the side, her right hand reaching her collarbone, the illusion of thin cloth beginning above the breast and ending just above the knee. Her left hand gently supporting the body of the snake - that is wrapped from her feet, beneath one knee, and around her mid-waist. It’s head facing forward as if guarding her, by her bared neck. It is worn, heavily so in some places, with a few chips that she laments but has made no attempts to repair; believing that the Mistress would be disappointed in her for trying to fool herself by fighting decay. It is her most precious item, and she would protect it with her life - believing it to be intertwined with her spirit and that of the Mistress. It has no traditional base, and is meant to rest on a simple wooden stand with 2 hooks to cradle the reeds on the side.

The Exile

The caravan to Asgalun was the furthest west she had ever traveled — a trade mission to a wealthy baron, carrying lilies, carved idols, and tamed beasts. Among the party was a man she had known since girlhood, Setahar, a member of a neighboring clan who had grown close with her family through years of visits to the Derketo shrine. Most who knew them assumed he would one day take her hand. She trusted him with the ease of long familiarity. She did not think him capable of what followed.

He had stolen from the baron — a small, careful theft of jewels and heirlooms, hidden beneath a false bottom in the saddlebags of one of the caravan's camels. When a guard discovered the goods as they readied to depart, Setahar's response was immediate. His eyes moved from panic to accusation in the span of a breath, only she would have noticed, and he turned it to her. What is the meaning of this? He cried out, fury burning over accusation, completely hiding the fear she saw. She immediately realized the gravity of the situation - and felt something between them snap. In this moment, for her, it was about survival. It felt like staring down a lioness that you just spotted stalking you in the brush, only this time, the threat was societal; it was human.

She told the truth to clear her name. It did not save her. None in her band would stand up against Setahar and the word of a guard who saw something he could not have seen - none would risk their own name to clear hers. The western cities of Shem had little patience for an independent woman from the east, a devotee of Derketo, traveling without a husband's sanction. There would be no trial by combat to defend her honor — that right was not extended to women in those courts. In the end she was the most probable suspect, and someone had to fall. He had chosen her deliberately, and with calculation.

She has not seen him since the day they were taken in for questioning, still wearing the expression of a man who genuinely suspected her. It was near 6 months till her fate was sealed and she was taken from Asgalun to Exile; half a year of being haunted by uncertainty, haunted by thoughts that circled her mind and gnawed at her bones, haunted by Setehar's face. She does not speak of how she felt about him. She tells the truth of the accusation willingly — knowing that in the Exiled Lands, everyone arrives wearing a reason, and she would rather hers be known than assumed. But what it cost her to look at his face in that courtyard and understand what he was doing — that she keeps.

Appearance

Build & Bearing

Gracefully built, standing near five feet and eleven inches. Lean and powerful muscle beneath deceiving softness — longer than average in neck, torso, limb, and hand, granting a tall and unhurried elegance. She is well aware that her height and features can intimidate some, and her every movement betrays the confidence she has in her form.

Adornment

She paints thin, intricate lines across her features in muted dark green — accent rather than mask. No tattoos. A leather and metal headdress bearing Derketo's imagery, its centre a lotus. Sandals that leave much of the foot bare, as though she prefers to feel the air and earth beneath her.

Face & Colouring

Warm tan skin. A small mark on her face where she felt the bite of stone beneath her left eye, a knife line on her right arm from a barfly in Shumir - near the mark of camel teeth, her shins and calves showing the stories of one that traveled in the desert and worked closely with beasts. Black hair that shines almost blue in certain light, worn loose or knotted in ornate styles, decorated with beads for occasion. Eyes a near-black dark grey with flecks of earthen colour and a striking true-black ring between the iris layers. A hooked nose she takes much pride in. Her teeth are healthy, white, symmetrical with slightly large central incisors.

Garb

At camp her preference is for traditional Shemite dress — airy, light fabric, dyed and vaguely ornate, embroidered with golden thread. Made for a desert climate and a woman unashamed of her body. The kind of clothing that suggests both comfort and occasion, that could pass through a pilgrim camp or a trade court without apology.

When traveling — reinforced leather guards her chest and arms, though fabric wraps the elbow, shoulders left free for movement. Her midrift still exposed, both for comfort of body, ease of movement for combat, and to draw strikes of the enemy in a way that she has learned to exploit. Her thighs also bearing armor beneath loose-fitted fabric.

In the Exiled Lands

She arrived recently, in rags like the rest. The bracelet is new on her wrist and the weight of what it means has not yet fully settled. She thinks of her family constantly, her fading father, her siblings entering adulthood without her. She dreams of the horse she gained in adulthood, Zaatar, and her serpent Zajee who she hopes lives on in the sands near Asgalun. She is bitter, and she is grieving, and she does not entirely hide either — but she carries both with a composure that comes from a lifetime of understanding that the world contains suffering as naturally as it contains beauty, and that neither cancels the other.

Her hope of return swells and recedes. There are days when she believes with every fiber that the bracelet can be removed, that she will find a way home before the people she loves become strangers to her. There are more days when that hope feels like something she is tending carefully, deliberately, because she cannot afford to let it die.

When the faith of freedom wavers, and she cannot make out it's shape, she builds. The Weeping Lotus will become her answer to everything the Exiled Lands take from those who wear the bracelet — a sanctuary, a place of belonging, of comfort and companionship, of lotus and warmth and the reclamation of something human. She has done it before, after a fashion. She knows what it feels like to live inside a community, the identity of her clan. She remembers it exactly. That memory is what she is working from.

She extends warmth readily, and is not naive about the cost of misplacing it. To her it is as currency, perhaps the most valuable, for guile can hide easiest within the guise of being guiless. She will tell you who she is and how she came to be here. She will not tell you everything. She is Shemitish, and has the awareness of someone raised in a culture that builds hospitality and wariness in the same breath — that can discern between the traveler who deserves rest and the one who deserves an arrow.

She is, above all, someone who loves the world without illusion about it, for all it's beauty and it's grit, and who intends — by the Mistress's grace and her own considerable determination — to find her way back to what she came from; to somewhere death is not corrupted and hers will be felt.
